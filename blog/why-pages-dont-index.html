Why Pages Don't Get Indexed (and How to Fix It)

"Discovered — currently not indexed." "Crawled — currently not indexed." If you've spent time in Google Search Console, you know these statuses. Here are the twelve most common causes, ordered by how often we see them across millions of submissions — with the fix for each.

1. Thin or duplicative content

The single biggest cause. Google increasingly declines to index pages that add little beyond what's already indexed. Fix: consolidate near-duplicates, add unique value (data, images, expertise) or noindex low-value pages deliberately.

2. Weak internal linking

Orphan pages — reachable only via sitemap — signal low importance. Fix: link every important page from at least two crawlable pages, ideally from high-traffic hubs.

3. Blocked by robots.txt

A Disallow rule prevents crawling entirely (and the page can still appear as a URL-only result). Fix: audit robots.txt; remember blocking crawling is not the same as preventing indexing.

4. Accidental noindex

Staging leftovers are notorious: a <meta name="robots" content="noindex"> or X-Robots-Tag header shipped to production. Fix: add a deploy-time check that fails the build if noindex appears on indexable templates.

5. Canonical pointing elsewhere

If your canonical tag points to a different URL, Google indexes that one instead. Fix: ensure canonicals are self-referencing on unique pages, and watch for protocol/trailing-slash mismatches.

6. Redirect chains and soft 404s

Chains of 3+ redirects waste budget; pages returning 200 with "not found" content get classified as soft 404s. Fix: redirect directly to the final URL; return real 404/410 codes for gone content.

7. Slow server response

When TTFB creeps above ~600ms, Google reduces crawl rate to protect your server. Fix: cache aggressively, use a CDN and keep p95 TTFB under 500ms.

8. JavaScript-only content

Content that appears only after client-side rendering waits in the render queue and sometimes never renders correctly. Fix: SSR or prerender critical content.

9. Crawl budget exhaustion

Faceted navigation, infinite calendars and parameter permutations can consume millions of crawls. Fix: see our crawl budget guide.

10. Low site-wide quality signals

A large ratio of thin/indexable pages drags down the whole domain's indexing rate. Fix: prune ruthlessly; a smaller, higher-quality index footprint lifts everything.

11. New site, no authority

Brand-new domains index slowly because crawl demand is low. Fix: direct submission (IndexNow reflects in hours), a few quality backlinks and consistent publishing shortens the trust-building phase.

12. Never actually submitted

Surprisingly common: the sitemap is stale, lastmod never updates and nobody pinged anyone. Fix: automate submission on publish — that's precisely what IndexJet's sitemap monitoring and API do.

A Diagnostic Order That Works

  1. URL Inspection in Search Console — what does Google say?
  2. Check robots.txt, meta robots, X-Robots-Tag and canonical
  3. Fetch the page as Googlebot (or curl) — status code, TTFB, rendered content
  4. Assess content uniqueness honestly
  5. Submit through official channels and monitor per-engine results
